Activity 1: Tidal Power Plant for Coastal Village

Power Generation: 4 cycles per day (2 high tides, 2 low tides)

System Explanation

Source of Energy: The system harnesses kinetic and potential energy from tidal movements caused by gravitational forces of the moon and sun. Coastal areas with significant tidal ranges (difference between high and low tide) are ideal locations.

Conversion Process: A barrage or dam structure is built across a tidal estuary, creating a basin. During high tide, sluice gates open to fill the basin. When the tide recedes, the gates close, creating a head difference. Water is then released through low-head turbines (typically bulb or Kaplan type), which rotate due to water pressure. These turbines are connected to generators that convert mechanical energy into electrical energy through electromagnetic induction.

Output and Utilization: The system generates AC electricity (typically 11 kV), which is stepped up via transformers for transmission to the coastal village grid. Optional battery storage systems can store excess energy during peak generation for use during slack tide periods, ensuring continuous power supply.

Real-World Application: Tidal power is highly predictable compared to solar and wind energy, making it reliable for baseload power in coastal communities. The La Rance Tidal Power Station in France (240 MW) and the Sihwa Lake Tidal Power Station in South Korea (254 MW) are successful examples. For a coastal village, this technology provides sustainable, emission-free electricity while supporting local fishing and tourism industries with minimal environmental impact when properly designed.

System Explanation

Source of Energy: The system utilizes cattle dung and agricultural waste as biomass feedstock. A dairy farm with 100 cattle produces approximately 1000-1200 kg of manure daily, which contains organic matter rich in carbon and nutrients suitable for anaerobic digestion.

Conversion Process: The collected cattle waste is mixed with water in a 1:1 ratio in the pre-treatment unit to create slurry. This slurry is fed into an anaerobic digester (typically fixed dome or floating drum type), where methanogenic bacteria break down organic matter in oxygen-free conditions at 30-35¬∞C. The process produces biogas (55-65% methane, 30-40% CO‚ÇÇ, and traces of H‚ÇÇS) over a retention period of 30-40 days. The biogas passes through a purification unit to remove hydrogen sulfide and carbon dioxide, improving its calorific value to approximately 5000-5500 kcal/m¬≥.

Output and Utilization: The purified biogas can be used for multiple purposes: cooking fuel (replacing LPG), electricity generation through biogas generators (approximately 10-15 kW capacity), and heating applications for farm operations. The spent slurry exits as high-quality organic bio-fertilizer rich in nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, which can be used for crop cultivation or sold as additional revenue.

Real-World Application: This system addresses waste management, energy needs, and fertilizer requirements simultaneously. Similar systems are successfully operating in dairy farms across India (Amul dairy cooperatives), Netherlands, and Germany. The dairy farm becomes energy self-sufficient, reduces greenhouse gas emissions (methane that would otherwise escape to atmosphere), and creates a circular economy by converting waste into valuable energy and fertilizer, improving farm profitability and sustainability.

System Explanation

Source of Energy: The system taps into geothermal energy from hot water reservoirs located 1-3 km below the earth's surface. These reservoirs exist in regions with volcanic activity or tectonic plate boundaries where the earth's geothermal gradient is higher, maintaining water temperatures between 150-180¬∞C. The heat originates from radioactive decay of minerals and residual heat from earth's formation.

Conversion Process: Production wells extract hot geothermal water using submersible pumps. In a binary cycle system, this hot water passes through a heat exchanger where it transfers thermal energy to a secondary working fluid (typically organic compounds like isobutane or isopentane with low boiling points). The working fluid vaporizes and expands through a turbine, causing rotation. The turbine drives a synchronous generator that produces three-phase AC electricity at 11 kV. After passing through the turbine, the working fluid vapor enters a condenser where it's cooled and liquefied (using air or water cooling), then recycled back to the heat exchanger. The cooled geothermal water is reinjected into the reservoir through injection wells, maintaining reservoir pressure and sustainability.

Output and Utilization: The system generates continuous baseload electricity with a capacity factor of 90-95%, significantly higher than intermittent sources like solar or wind. The generated electricity at 11 kV is stepped up to transmission voltage (66 kV or higher) via transformers and fed into the regional grid. Excess heat from the cooled geothermal water can be utilized for district heating, greenhouse agriculture, or industrial processes.

Real-World Application: Binary cycle plants are ideal for moderate-temperature geothermal resources and have minimal environmental impact with near-zero emissions. Successful implementations include the Mammoth Lakes plant in California, Hellishei√∞i Power Station in Iceland, and Wayang Windu in Indonesia. These plants provide reliable, renewable baseload power with a small land footprint. For regions with hot water wells, geothermal energy offers energy independence, economic development through job creation, and a pathway to carbon neutrality. The technology is mature, proven, and can operate 24/7 regardless of weather conditions, making it an excellent complement to variable renewable sources.
